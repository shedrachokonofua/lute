Yet Another Great Fela Album
No matter what your taste is, it’s very hard to dislike Fela. His songs are so fun to listen to & this is no exception.

He takes some jazz & funk influence, with the increased focus on keyboard & saxophone. “Igbe” has a nice tenor sax solo & the horn flourishes on “Fefe Naa Efe” add a smoother feel to this tropical sounding piece. The guitar tone on that song is nice too, it’s super clean & bright.

This is a brisk listen too, being only 31 minutes long. Despite the fact that there’s only 3 songs, I feel like I got enough out of it. Most of Fela’s albums are on the shorter side, but I always leave feeling satisfied with my experience.

8.75/10

Kuti's Greatest Achievement
(Review #51, May 6th [2021])

Note: This review is the third part of a series in which I review a single album by each of the current year's Rock and Roll Hall of Fame nominees.

Gentleman is the eight overall album by Nigerian musician and afrobeat pioneer Fela Kuti alongside his backing band The Africa '70. Afrobeat has and will most likely forever continue to be an obscure genre. With that in mind, I am completely shocked by Fela and Co.'s ability to maintain a decent sized fanbase even into the 21st century. However, it isn't hard to see why he is such a beloved figure.

First of all, the musicianship presented here is incredibly impressive. It is quite obvious that both Fela Kuti and Africa '70 were beginning to take their most major inspirations from western music at this point in their career. Influences from artists such as Charlie Parker and the more experimental side of Miles Davis are quite obvious throughout this album. In fact, there is even electric piano work of Kuti's on tracks like "Igbe (Na Shit)." However, the group still managed to keep their traditional Nigerian influences. This is most prominently seen in the excellent drumming done throughout Gentleman on tracks like "Fefe Naa Efe."

The entire band sounds great here. The drumming, as stated before, is on point. Fela Kuti's tenor/alto saxophone and electric piano additions are both great. Tunde Williams really shines with the trumpet on the tracks "Gentleman" and "Igbe (Na Shit)." Every musician on this album truly comes together to make something amazing. There isn't a single moment on Gentleman where the instrumentals let the listener down. "Gentleman" is arguably the best track here due to it's length, but both of the other tracks are equally great.

One of my favorite things about Gentleman, and the majority of Africa 70's discography, is the vocals of the group's frontman. Fela Kuti is by no means a great vocalist. Most of his singing is done either in a talk-singing or yell-singing style. However, when the incredibly political and serious messages of the album are taken into account, it gives the whole release a sense of urgency.

I also think that both the production and mixing here sound quite good. Each individual musician and instrument truly stand out on Gentleman. Take, for example, the masterpiece title track. It is quite a long tune with a number of parts and instruments throughout it. However, they all manage to stand out on their own. The great production on this album further solidifies as possibly the strong album by Kuti and Africa '70.

In conclusion, Gentleman is possibly Fela Kuti and Africa '70's greatest achievement. It may only be three songs, but it truly packs a punch. Every single little factor here works. It really is a shame that Afrobeat as a genre doesn't get more attention outside of Kuti. However, if there was one artist from the genre that deserves appreciation, it is most definitely him.
